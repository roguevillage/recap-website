Re-Imagining Cryptography and Privacy (ReCAP) Workshop


The Re-Imagining Cryptography and Privacy (ReCAP) Workshop expands the intentional space of the Community-Driven Cryptography Project, which explores the ways in which cryptography and privacy intersect with society toward designing, creating, and sustaining technologies that explicitly benefit marginalized communities. The focus of the ReCAP Workshop is Re-Imagination: identifying the aspects of cryptography and privacy that contribute to marginalization, and generating new approaches, ideas, and designs that center marginalized voices, resist toxic aspects of technology production, and leverage cryptography and privacy tools toward dismantling systems of marginalization. Like the Community-Driven Cryptography Project, the ReCAP Workshop seeks to build and sustain an interdisciplinary community of cryptographers, social scientists, humanities scholars, community organizations, industry practitioners, and technologists, and contribute to the broadening of access to this field.

ReCAP 2024

ReCAP 2024 is a hybrid workshop from May 2-3 that will take place physically at Boston Unviersity in Boston, Massachusetts (USA) and virtually on Zoom. Unfortunately we do not have stipends available for physical attendance this year, but hope to secure funding in the future.

Session Topics

  • Cryptography and Privacy in Context: Cryptography and privacy have deep and under-explored historical, social, political, environmental, psychological, emotional, and spiritual dimensions. The art of hiding and transmitting private information in plain sight has persisted throughout the social, political, and spiritual histories of people; cryptographic protocols like the African National Congress' Operation Vula have been key components of anti-apartheid and social justice movements; living in environments with pervasive surveillance has been shown to have profound negative effects on the human psyche. This session creates space for the presentation and discussion of histories, stories, artifacts, reflections, and ideas related to the many dimensions of cryptography and privacy.
  • Surveillance and Systems of Marginalization: Much of the work in cryptography and privacy aims to prevent or at least confine the scope of surveillance. While there are many forms of surveillance which disproportionately impact marginalized people, norms in the field of cryptography and privacy focus on privacy rights for citizens of a particular nation, capital, and intellectual property, framing cryptographic tools as protection against hackers and large governmental organizations like the United States' National Security Agency (NSA). This session centers forms of surveillance explicitly wielded against marginalized people: the surveillance systems of police, local law enforcement, and domestic federal agencies such as the United States' Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) and Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE), intimate partner, family, and peer surveillance, and the surveillance systems in schools and education technologies, healthcare, and welfare systems. Work in this session might document, discuss, or develop new analysis at the intersections of systems of surveillance and systems of marginalization.
  • Cryptography and Privacy for the People: How do we think about, talk about, design, build, and sustain cryptography and privacy tools that meet the needs of individuals and groups of people who are the most vulnerable to surveillance and marginalization? How might existing practices of threat modeling and protocol design in cryptography and privacy flatten, miss, or undermine their needs? This session considers the lived privacy needs and practices of marginalized people, as well as mechanisms for co-creating cryptography and privacy tools that reflect these needs and practices.
  • Cryptography and Privacy in the Margins: This session creates space for acknowledging and confronting the difficulties that people with certain intersections of identity (Black, Brown, and Indigenous People of Color, people from the Global South, people without caste privilege, working-class people, women, trans and queer people, Muslim people and members of religious and ethnic minorities, disabled and neurodivergent people, people who speak languages other than English) experience in the field of cryptography and privacy, and conversely, acknowledging and confronting the power that people with opposing intersections of identity hold over decision-making in the field. It will center conversations surrounding challenges, new perspectives, and action steps toward not only equity of access and representation, but also toward direct input, influence, and impact of marginalized voices at the highest levels of decision-making bodies and processes in the field.
  • Cryptography, Privacy, and Creative Expression: Engaging in creative expression and other joyful activities are essential to our well-being as people who work in cryptography and privacy, and who occupy computer science spaces in general. This session is dedicated to all forms of creative expression (multi-media art, music, song, dance, poetry and creative writing, performance, etc.) produced and enjoyed by people in the cryptography and privacy community.
  • (Meta Topic) Dreaming and Design: This meta session is dedicated to discussing the theory and practice of community-driven work, as well as building explicit processes around co-ideation and co-design in cryptography and privacy. What does "for the people" and "community" mean in our context? What does it mean and look like for the theory and practice of cryptography and privacy to be "community-driven"? What does the ideation and design process of "cryptography for the people" look like? Session participants might highlight, unpack, and analyze particular positive examples of co-design, or take a "big-picture" approach to imagining what this process could look like in an ideal world. We will focus on definitions and process past, present, and future, and draw on the experiences and expertise of scholar-activists across fields.

Submit

Submissions to ReCAP 2024 will be accepted on a rolling basis until March 31, 2024. All submissions that fit the workshop topic(s) will be included in the workshop proceedings (published on the ReCAP website and as a printable zine), and as many submissions as possible will be included in the workshop program (depending on desired presentation format and volume of submissions per topic). To submit an idea, please use this link.

Submission Formats

  • Standard Talk: A traditional format with a short talk and time for Q&A.
  • 5-and-5 Session: A maximum 5-minute talk or presentation, plus a 5-minute Q&A, discussion, or short activity. Good for posing questions and starting a discussion.
  • 10-and-10 Session: A maximum 10-minute talk or presentation, plus a 10-minute Q&A, discussion, or short activity. Good for a short introduction with time for discussion and collective processing.
  • Short Activity (10-25m): A short block where the primary focus of the presenter(s) is to introduce and facilitate a group activity (the structure of the groups is up to you--we will give you an estimated number of in-person/virtual attendance as soon as we know). We encourage folks planning a longer activity to work with collaborators who can help facilitate in the space (including in-person and virtual components).
  • Long Activity (30-45m): Same as above, except the block of time is longer.
  • Creative Expression Session: A variable-length block where the primary focus of the presenter(s) is either to present or perform original creative work, or to lead participants in a creative activity.
  • Stationary Artifact: A visual, audio-visual, audio, or multimedia artifact that will be displayed as part of in the artifact display.
  • Other Submissions: If none of the above formats fit your submission, please describe the format that would work best for what you have in mind (in particular the time, space, and any other requirements you have). We will find an appropriate block for you based on the constraints of your submission.
